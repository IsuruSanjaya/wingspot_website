Literature Survey

Effective conservation of avian species, particularly in isolated and ecologically sensitive regions such as Adam’s Bridge in Sri Lanka, requires innovative monitoring and identification methods. Existing research has leveraged Internet of Things (IoT), LoRa, machine learning, and computer vision technologies to address the challenges posed by traditional methods.

The use of IoT and LoRa (Long Range) technologies in ecological monitoring has proven highly effective in overcoming the limitations of remote monitoring. LoRa’s ability to transmit data over long distances with low power consumption has been demonstrated in various domains, including environmental monitoring, smart agriculture, and industrial automation. For instance, studies have shown that LoRa-based networks efficiently facilitate real-time data collection even in regions with limited internet connectivity, making them a suitable choice for wildlife monitoring in remote areas [1][2]. Additionally, the integration of deep learning techniques like YOLO (You Only Look Once) has enhanced the accuracy of object detection in such applications, enabling precise identification of bird species and behavior in real-time [3].

In regions like Adam’s Bridge, the identification of both known and unknown bird species presents a significant challenge due to limited access and biodiversity complexity. Previous research on few-shot learning and zero-shot learning has shown promising results in classifying unseen categories of bird species by leveraging minimal training data. For example, large-scale studies have successfully applied zero-shot learning for classifying zoological illustrations, while community-driven projects like eBird have employed citizen science approaches to enhance biodiversity research through public participation and data sharing [4][5]. However, a gap remains in developing methods that facilitate collaborative identification of unknown bird species using social media platforms and comment analysis through natural language processing (NLP) [6].

The analysis of avian behavior is crucial for understanding how environmental changes impact species and for developing effective conservation strategies. Traditional methods for observing bird behavior have often been manual, labor-intensive, and susceptible to human error, leading to inconsistencies in data collection. To address these challenges, recent studies have introduced multi-modal classification systems that integrate audio and visual data to improve behavioral analysis. For instance, Lin et al. developed a dual feature-rates deep fusion network, combining audio and visual cues to enhance bird posture detection and behavior recognition [7]. Similar multi-modal fusion techniques have also been applied to analyze complex flocking behaviors and environmental influences, demonstrating the potential for integrated data approaches in advancing wildlife research [8].

Recent advancements in deep learning and computer vision have significantly improved the monitoring of avian behavior, particularly in areas like incubation and hatching success, which have traditionally relied on labor-intensive manual observation methods prone to human error [9]. YOLO models, such as YOLOv5, have shown notable effectiveness in real-time object detection, offering high-speed processing and accuracy in identifying bird species in complex environments [10][11]. However, while most research has focused primarily on species identification, behavior classification and reproductive success analysis remain underexplored. Studies using machine learning in animal behavior, such as those by Valletta et al. and Rivas et al., have often overlooked integrating behavior classification with reproductive outcomes, highlighting an opportunity for more automated and precise analysis using deep learning [12][13].

In conclusion, the integration of IoT, LoRa, machine learning, and computer vision technologies presents a comprehensive solution to address the challenges of avian conservation. These approaches not only enable remote monitoring and real-time behavioral analysis but also foster community involvement in identifying and protecting endangered bird species. By combining technological advancements with collaborative efforts, this research contributes to the overarching goal of preserving fragile ecosystems and supporting biodiversity conservation.

Research Problem & Solution

Research Problem

How can we make monitoring endangered birds on remote islands more efficient, considering the practical challenges of travel and accessibility?.

How can we prevent the unintended introduction of foreign species during monitoring, ensuring the safety of the endangered birds?

How can we accurately identify the species and birds who have never been seen before ?

How can we observe nesting and egg-laying behaviour without causing disruptions or altering their natural patterns?

Research Solution

Research Objectives

Monitoring and Detection of Endangered Birds Using Deep Learning Methods via an IoT Device

Our solution uses a camouflaged IoT device combined with LoRa technology to monitor birds in real-time on remote islands. Powered by the YOLOv8 algorithm, the device can detect and identify bird species, sending information directly to a mobile app. This approach helps protect endangered species by offering a reliable way to monitor wildlife in areas where traditional connectivity is limited.

Unidentified Bird Species Identification and Community Engagement

The IoT device captures bird images and uses Few-Shot Learning with a CLIP model to classify them as either a red-wattled lapwing, Indian thick-knee, black-winged stilt, or an unknown species. If the bird is unknown, the image is analyzed by the OpenAI Multimodal Vision API for a possible name and uploaded to the WingSpot Facebook group for further identification by bird researchers. Comments from the group are scraped using Apify, and a Named Entity Recognition (NER) model analyzes the feedback. The final bird name is determined by comparing the API results with the community's input.

Multi-Modal Behavior Classification Using Audio Visual Data for Endangered Bird Species

Video footage of the Red Wattled Lapwing is split into frames and processed using an InceptionResNet CNN model, which classifies each frame and determines the dominant class based on the majority of frames. Simultaneously, the audio from the video is analyzed using a combined YamNet and logistic regression model. The final output is generated by combining the video and audio results with weightages of 0.4 for video classification and 0.6 for audio classification

Automated Behavioral Monitoring and Hatching Success Prediction for Bird Conservation

Automated behavioral monitoring and hatching success calculation for bird conservation. Using the YOLOv8 object detection model, the system detects birds, eggs, and hatchlings from video footage, spatial coordinates and bounding box labels for each detection. It calculates the proximity between birds and egg clusters, classifying behaviors such as incubation, attention, and unattended. The system tracks the duration and percentage of time spent in each behavior across multiple frames. It also records the initial egg count and the final hatchling count to compute the egg-hatching success rate after 30 days. All results, including behavior classifications, time durations, counts, and the hatching success rate, are displayed to the user for further analysis.

Methodology


The bird monitoring mobile app leverages IoT, AI, and behavioral analysis to enhance bird research on Adam’s Bridge Island. Camouflaged IoT devices capture high-resolution images of birds and transmit metadata (e.g., time and location) using LoRa modules. These images are processed by a YOLOv8 model for real-time bird detection, while a Few-Shot Learning model (CLIP) classifies known species (Red-wattled lapwing, Indian thick-knee, black-winged stilt) and for unknown birds, the image is first analyzed by the OpenAI Multimodal Vision API to suggest a possible name. Simultaneously, the image is uploaded to a dedicated Facebook group for bird researchers and enthusiasts, where users can provide further identification through comments. These comments are then scraped using Apify, and a Named Entity Recognition (NER) model analyzes them. The results from both the API and community feedback are compared to determine the final name of the bird, which is then displayed for the user.

Additionally, the app performs multi-modal behavioral analysis by combining video and audio data to detect the behavioral states (agitated or relaxed) of red-wattled lapwings. Yolov8 and InceptionResNetv2 models detect birds and classify visual behaviors respectively, while YamNet combined with logic Regression processes audio cues, with a fusion of both outputs for accuracy. In addition to the broader capabilities of the app, the system employs YOLOv8 to detect eggs, hatchlings, and birds, classifying behaviors like Incubation, Attention, and Unattended based on proximity. It tracks behavior durations and logs egg and hatchling counts, calculating egg hatching success over time. These metrics are then made accessible to users through the mobile app interface, providing a comprehensive tool for monitoring and analyzing avian reproductive behaviors, supporting both researchers and bird enthusiasts in their efforts to monitor and protect bird species.
